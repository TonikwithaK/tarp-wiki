# Clan Arena

Clan Arena (CA) is Warfork's team elimination mode. The last team with at least one player alive wins the round. A match runs for a set number of rounds: typically best of 11 or a fixed count depending on server settings: and the team that wins the most rounds takes the match.

It is the primary competitive team format in Warfork.

---

## Core Rules

Every player spawns with the full weapon loadout and a substantial ammo reserve. There are no item pickups during a round: no Mega Health, no armor packs, nothing to scavenge. The health and armor you spawn with is all you have unless your server's configuration provides regeneration. Once it's gone, survival depends on not getting hit.

Self-damage from rockets and grenades is disabled. This is specific to Warfork's CA implementation and matters in practice: you can fire rockets at close range, into doorways, or at players you're chasing without punishing yourself. It removes an entire class of risk from aggressive play.

When a player dies, they spectate their team for the remainder of the round. A round ends the moment one team is entirely eliminated.

---

## Why Ammo Isn't the Concern

Because everyone spawns loaded, ammunition management is a non-issue for most of a round. You can fire freely, swap weapons freely, and play aggressively without counting shots. This shifts the cognitive load entirely onto positioning, routing, and target focus: which is where CA gets its depth.

The ammo abundance also means there's no incentive to camp weapon pickups mid-round and no power disparity between players on the same team from unequal equipment. CA is as close to a pure combat test as Warfork offers at the team level.

---

## Map Design and Movement

CA maps are built with teleporters, multi-level geometry, and vertical routes. Understanding where the teleporters go and how quickly they can carry you across the map is essential knowledge for both attackers and defenders. A player who doesn't know the map is easy to isolate and delete; a player who does can rotate, escape, and reposition in ways that look like magic to opponents who haven't done the work.

[[Movement]]: specifically strafe-jumping and wall-jumping: is what makes teleporter routes fast and flanks viable. Getting to a position first, arriving at an angle your opponents don't expect, or disengaging before a 2v1 collapses on you all depend on moving efficiently. A team that moves well covers more ground, holds better angles, and can reset into defensive setups that the other team has to break.

The primary competitive CA map is **wfca1**. Learn its teleporter connections and the sightlines that control each section. Most high-level CA play on that map traces back to a few contested chokepoints.

---

## Round Strategy

CA rounds are won by information, coordination, and numbers. A 3v3 that becomes a 3v2 is a significant advantage; a 3v2 that becomes a 3v1 is usually over. Every early death is a countdown.

**Stay together: until you shouldn't.** The baseline is moving as a group so that any engagement is a numbers advantage. Splitting up to control multiple areas makes sense only when your team has the coordination to re-merge before the enemy collapses one of your subgroups.

**Identify the feed.** If one player on your team is repeatedly dying first, that player is the problem the other team has already solved. Either that player needs a safer role, or the team needs to protect them differently. Don't let a 3v3 become a 2v3 in the first ten seconds of a round.

**Teleporter discipline.** Teleporters are fast but committed: you can't stop mid-travel. If the enemy is holding the exit of a teleporter, you'll arrive directly into their crosshairs. Vary your routes. Force the enemy to cover multiple approaches.

**Area denial.** The Rocket Launcher and Grenade Launcher are effective for area denial. You don't need to kill someone with a direct hit if you can consistently prevent them from holding a position.

---

## Competitive Format

The standard competitive format for Warfork CA is **3v3**. This is the format used by organized tournaments including The Keep, which ran on a double-elimination bracket. 3v3 is small enough that every player's individual performance is visible and critical, but large enough that team coordination creates real separation between squads.

In 3v3, losing one player immediately puts your team on the back foot. The dynamics are faster and less forgiving than larger team formats: which is part of what makes it compelling to play and watch.
